Facebook or Fakebook? The social media giant is at risk of breaking its own business model

By Alexis Dormandy Tech business Last updated: August 8th, 2012

39 Comments Comment on this article

Last week was quite a week at Facebook. Their share price dropped below $20 (from $38 at float); it turns out that nearly 10 per cent of Facebook’s users are fake (anyone who was surprised by this hasn’t been using the service); and a journalist managed to get 3000 Likes in a day for Virtual Bagels, a totally fictional business that didn’t do anything.

Customer growth and Likes, the engine of Facebook’s advertising product, continue to grow unabated, but as the business has moved from selling a dream to delivering the numbers, the microscope is on its business model.

Facebook is an advertising business, and whatever it says in public, its customers are businesses. We consumers are the product they are selling.

The 83 million fake accounts don’t affect users, but the issue is the degree to which those fake accounts are the ones liking products or even clicking ads. On that front a worrying piece of news came from a small business, Limited Run, that is closing down its Facebook page having estimated that only 20 per cent of the clicks on its ads came from real customers. Tom Mango, the cofounder, explained:  “80 per cent of clicks we were paying for were from bots. That’s correct. Bots were loading pages and driving up our advertising costs.”

Announcing that almost 10 per cent of users are fake is one thing, but if fake users are responsible for a large proportion of the clicks on ads, that’s horrendous news for Facebook’s business model.

Similarly, the ease with which they were acquired for a fictional business spells trouble for Facebook’s consumer users. The issue is those 3,000 people who Liked the fake Virtual Bagels business. If you believe Facebook’s social model, then 450,000 people believe their friends have recommended the purchase of "virtual bagels" to them. If people are prepared to Like a fake business, what is the value of a Like to a friend? Very little – and that undermines everything.

My business, LoveThis, recently analysed the Facebook Likes of its customers to see whether people would actually recommend things they’ve liked to their friends. Turns out the answer is that Facebook users only recommend 13 per cent of their Likes – that’s 87 per cent of Likes that people wouldn’t actually put their name behind. It’s also notable that the majority of those that make the cut are for relatively low-value categories like music, celebrities and films rather than products, hotels, restaurants and activities. And it appears that a very small number of people are carrying out most of the activity – only 2 per cent of users are responsible for 30 per cent of Likes.

All of this suggests that users assign very little credibility to their own Likes, and don’t expect their friends to trust them either.

The web is a seething mass of activity, and within it any marketing system that can be manipulated is going to be manipulated. At the heart of Facebook are Likes – they are the thing that allow advertisers to target their advertising, and it is the targeting of customers that keeps marketing costs down.

The danger for Facebook is twofold: firstly the manipulations of fake customers and fake Likes undermine its credibility in selling targeted advertising, and even more importantly if Facebook Likes loses their credibility with consumers, then it becomes nothing more than a 21st century mailing list. That loss of credibility would be very difficult to reclaim.
